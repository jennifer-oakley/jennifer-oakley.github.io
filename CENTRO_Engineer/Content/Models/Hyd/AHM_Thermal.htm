About thermal hydraulics

Based on the current hydraulics model needs, pressure and temperature are calculated separately. A special energy equation, which relates the fluid and material properties to temperature profiles along the wellbore, govern temperature distribution along the wellbore. This equation only considers the conservation of internal energy. Since the equations for conservation of momentum have already been solved, the conservation of mechanical energy has been removed.

The hydraulic model considers the non-isothermal rheology and applies API RP 13-D to calculate annular friction accounting for the downhole temperature and pressure. Specific heat capacity and thermal conductivity depend on the current temperature. For this model, they are assumed to be constant.

Yield point

Politte concluded from his analysis of rheological data for emulsions that the yield point is not a strong function of pressure, and becomes progressively less as temperature increases. The effects of temperature on the yield point are, however, hard to predict, as there are chemical as well as particle effects that have to be considered.

Politte advises that in situations where it may be important to know the precise behavior of the drilling fluid, the yield point should be measured on a viscometer capable of such measurements. If the equipment is not available, he provides the following steps based on an empirical equation obtained from the analysis of diesel oil based drilling fluid (Politte 1985).

Heat transfer and heat generation

Heat transfer coefficients depend on flow-properties, material thermal properties, geometry, and the interface mediums of the neighboring conduits. The thermal equation is valid not only for fluid mediums but also for solid mediums and can be used to simulate the heat-transfer between adjacent conduits. This equation also provides a good framework for studying conjugate heat-transfer that corresponds to temperature calculations for a wellbore geometry.

Two different interfaces exist:

  • Solid-fluid interface is the heat transfer that occurs between a solid surface and a fluid, such as between the fluid in the drillstring and the drill pipe wall. Solid-fluid interfaces also occur between the annular fluid and the casing, as well as between the annular fluid and the formation. The heat transfer coefficient for a solid-fluid interface depends on the fluid properties and flow properties.
  • Solid-solid interface is the heat transfer that occurs between two solid surfaces, such as between the casing wall and the formation. The heat transfer coefficient for a solid-solid interface depends on the thermal conductivity of the material.

Heat transfer coefficients

The heat transfer coefficients described in this section are used to represent the combined convection and diffusion interactions between different radial regions of a wellbore. The wellbore components that play significant role in heat transfer include the drilling fluid, drillstring, casing, and formation.

To account for heat transfer, the temperature model requires additional layers outside of the circulating fluid. Each of these layers is called a shell, which represents a cylindrical section. These layers, from inside the drill pipe to the wellbore formation, include:

  • Drilling fluid in the drill pipe
  • Drill pipe steel
  • Drilling fluid in the annulus
  • Casing
  • Near formation
  • Far formation

Heat transfer, primarily conduction, also occurs in shells that do not contain a fluid. To account for heat transfer, these additional shells require thermal conductivity, specific heat capacity, and density.

Typical values for these additional material properties for different shells are as follows (Keller 1971):
