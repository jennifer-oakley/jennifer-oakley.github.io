About the fit predictor tool

Power section fit is probably the most critical factor that determines stator life. Optimum fit provides a balance between frictional losses, power section efficiency, and stator life. A proper power section assembly process involves selecting a fit at surface that will provide optimal stator life under expected downhole conditions.

The fit predictor tool on the Planning > HyperLine™ Toolbox > FIT Predictor page is used to evaluate the rotor-stator fit on a drilling motor. Results have proven to be accurate in applications with both low and high downhole circulating temperatures, all elastomer types, different drilling fluids, and the widest range of operational conditions. The fit predictor tool accounts for the effects of downhole conditions, system hydraulics, chemical reactions, heat build-up, wear rates, time, among many others factors to predict the power section fit accurately.

If possible, enter the in-shop fit measurements. You can either use the direct measurements taken before the job started or the measurements taken after a reline maintenance procedure.

To use the motor fit predictor tool:

  1. Go to the Planning > HyperLine™ Toolbox > FIT Predictor page.
  2. Enter the drilling motor properties, well information, drilling parameters, fluid information, and differential pressure limits. See "Inputs".
  3. Click the Calculate button.

The fit predictor results will be calculated for the drilling motor. The results will appear on the right side of the screen. See "Results".

Results

Rotor-stator fit

The Rotor-Stator Fit section includes the a table that shows the absolute fit of the rotor and stator at the start and end of the well, for each available size classification. Rotor-stator fit is determined by comparing the relative fit to lower and upper limits for the relative fit and the optimal relative fit. The relative fit is a percentage that represents the absolute fit divided by the thickness of the minor stator. The thickness of the minor is determined by the difference of the catalog tube ID of the stator and the shop dimensions of the stator minor diameter.

Rotor-stator fit is classified into the following categories:

  • Loose Fit – The relative fit is less than the lower limit of the relative fit.
  • Longer Lifetime – The relative fit is less than the lower limit of the optimal relative fit.
  • Lifetime + Performance – The relative fit is less than the upper limit of the optimal relative fit.
  • Max Performance – The relative fit is less than the upper limit of the relative fit.
  • Tight Fit – The relative fit exceeds the high limit of the relative fit.

The Rotor-Stator Fit section also includes the Power Section Evaluation widget, which graphs where the absolute fit at the start and end of the well falls within the absolute limits for the rotor and stator. Each limit range is color coded on the graph.

  • The dark blue area represents the lower range of the risky zone. The risky zone lower limit is based on the lower limit of the relative fit, adjusted to account for the thickness of the minor stator and the vector tool offset.
  • The light blue area represents the lower range of the acceptable zone. The acceptable zone lower limit is based on the lower limit of the relative fit, adjusted to account for the thickness of the minor stator and the vector tool offset.
  • The green area represents the optimal zone. The optimal zone limits are based on the limits of the optimal relative fit, adjusted to account for the thickness of the minor stator and the vector tool offset.
  • The yellow area represents the upper range of the acceptable zone. The acceptable zone upper limit is based on the upper limit of the relative fit, adjusted to account for the thickness of the minor stator and the vector tool offset.
  • The red area represents the upper range of the risky zone. The risky zone upper limit is based on the upper limit of the relative fit, adjusted to account for the thickness of the minor stator and the vector tool offset.

Details

When you expand the Details section, there are additional results available. The Change Of Fit tables show the change in fit at the start depth and the end depth.

Definitions for the Change of Fit tables
Row Name Definition
Fit On Surface Theoretical minor surface fit of the elastomer. If direct measurements are available, this value is calculated using the shop temperature. If not, it is calculated at 20 °C.
Thermal Expansion Change in minor fit due to thermal expansion. If direct measurements are available, this value is calculated using the shop temperature. If not, it is calculated at 20 °C.
Hysteresis Change in fit due to the hysteresis effect, which is caused by heat generated by the flexing of the stator lobes and the pressurized fluids.
Elastomer Swelling Change in fit due to elastomer swelling, based on the change in volume. The swelling effect is assumed to be negligible at the start depth. If the change in volume is unavailable, a table value will be used instead.
Hydraulics Change in fit due to the effects of hydraulics, including bit pressure drop, average flow velocity, and overall pressure.
Rotor Wear Change in fit due to rotor wear, based on the rotor wear rate and the theoretical changes in minor and major fit due to thermal expansion. All wear effects are considered to be negligible at the start depth.
Stator Wear Change in fit due to stator wear, based on the average stator wear rate. All wear effects are considered to be negligible at the start depth.
Change Of Fit Total change in fit due to thermal expansion, elastomer swelling, hysteresis, hydraulics, rotor wear, and stator wear.
Absolute Fit Theoretical surface fit, plus the total change of fit
Relative Fit

Percentage representing the absolute fit divided by the thickness of the minor stator. The thickness of the minor is determined by the difference of the catalog tube ID of the stator and the shop dimensions of the stator minor diameter.
