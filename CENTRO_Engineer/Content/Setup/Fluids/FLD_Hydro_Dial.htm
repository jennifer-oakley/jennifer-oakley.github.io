About dial readings

The Dial Readings tab uses isothermal readings directly from a viscometer to calculate the rheological properties of a fluid. Dial readings can be used to define the rheology of any hydrocarbon, without calculating additional values such as PV or YP.

The rheological properties of the hydrocarbon are calculated using one dial reading for the fluid. Viscometer readings are used to calculate the hydrocarbon's plastic viscosity, yield point, yield stress, consistency factor, and flow index. Gel strength readings are used to determine the viscosity of the fluid when the fluid isn't flowing.

The calculated rheological properties are shown below the dial readings. These calculations are based on the dial readings and the selected rheological model. To use a different rheological model, select one of the options in the Rheology Fit menu.

The rheology of cements and spacers is measured using different viscometers than drilling muds. Additional viscometer readings are taken at 60 rpm and 30 rpm. It is not always possible to measure a reading at 600 rpm, due to the high density of cements.

Entering dial readings

To enter dial readings properties for a new hydrocarbon:

  1. Go to the Setup > Fluids > Hydrocarbons page to add a new hydrocarbon. See "Adding hydrocarbons".
  2. Go to the Dial Readings tab on the right side of the screen
  3. In the θ600 - θ3 fields, enter the viscometer readings taken at each speed.
  4. In the Gel 10" - Gel 30' fields, enter the gel strength measured for each interval.
  5. In the Rheology Fit field, select the rheological model that is the closest fit for the hydrocarbon.
  6. Click the Save Changes button ().

When you save the hydrocarbon, it will be added to the CENTRO® Engineer database. When you run an analysis, you'll be able to select the new hydrocarbon in the analysis settings. However, you will not be able to edit the hydrocarbon properties or dial readings.

Field definitions

On the Dial Readings tab, the hydrocarbon's rheology is defined using the viscosity and gel strength readings listed below.

Field definitions for viscosity and gel strength readings
Field Name Definition
θ600 Viscometer reading at 600 rpm.
θ300

Viscometer reading at 300 rpm
θ200

Viscometer reading at 200 rpm
θ100

Viscometer reading at 100 rpm
θ6

Viscometer reading at 6 rpm
θ3

Viscometer reading at 3 rpm
Gel 10"

Gel strength after the hydrocarbon has set for ten seconds
Gel 30"

Gel strength after the hydrocarbon has set for 30 seconds
Gel 10'

Gel strength after the hydrocarbon has set for ten minutes
Gel 30'

Gel strength after the hydrocarbon has set for 30 minutes
Friction Multiplier

Multiplier used to account for friction reducers added to the hydrocarbon. A friction multiplier of 1 indicates that there are no friction reducers added to the hydrocarbon. Smaller multipliers indicate an increased reduction in friction. A typical value would be between 0.9 and 0.8. The friction multiplier is unlikely to be less than 0.6.
Rheology Fit Rheological model used to calculate the rheological properties

The table below lists the calculated rheological properties.

Calculated rheological properties
Field Name Definition
n Power law exponent, an indicator of the hydrocarbon's flow behavior. It is normally calculated from the 300 rpm and 600 rpm viscometer readings.
K Consistency factor, which relates the cohesion of the individual particles in a hydrocarbon, its ability to deform, and its resistance to flow.
τ0 Yield stress, the amount of stress that must be applied in order to make the hydrocarbon flow. Yield stress is normally measured at the 3 rpm viscometer reading.
PV Plastic viscosity, a measure of shear stress that indicates the hydrocarbon's resistance to flow.
YP Yield point, the minimum amount of shear stress required for the hydrocarbon to flow.
LSYP Low-shear yield point, the yield point of the hydrocarbon at a low shear rate.
Max Error Maximum amount of error that occurs for any point.
