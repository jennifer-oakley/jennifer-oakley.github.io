About the torque and drag model

The primary cause of torque and drag effects is friction. Friction is the force that resists the relative motion of solid surfaces, fluid layers, and material elements as they slide against one another.

A friction factor is used to account for friction, as well as other sources of torque and drag effects. In addition to friction, torque and drag effects may also be caused by downhole dynamics, formation swelling, wellbore ledges, key seats, fluids, and the cuttings bed.

For the purposes of the mathematical model, friction factor is a lumped dimensionless parameter and should not be confused with a conventional coefficient of friction. The coefficient of friction only accounts for torque and drag effects caused by friction. Using a lumped parameter for friction factor allows the mathematical model to include other sources of torque and drag effects that may be encountered downhole.

The mathematical models predict the torque and drag effects generated by the drillstring, based on how the drillstring deforms due to stresses in a well. The model applies a fourth-order explicit method to a system of ordinary differential equations for force and torque during static equilibrium. The resulting equations are the basis for estimating drillstring stresses. In Victus™ Operation, torque and drag analysis supports four models for predicting the effects of torque and drag. See "String models".
