About uncertainty ellipsoids

The uncertainty ellipsoid represents the amount of uncertainty of the precise location a particular point in the wellpath. These ellipses form an uncertainty envelope around the wellpath to approximate the shape of the wellpath.

When you use a definitive survey in an anti-collision analysis, the uncertainty ellipsoid is calculated based on the actual survey depth and the survey tool used to take the survey. The recommended max survey depth interval is 30 m (100 ft).

When you use a current plan, the uncertainty ellipsoid is calculated based on the ellipsoid spacing defined in the anti-collision parameters and the default survey tool. See "Anti-collision parameters". To change the default survey tool, right-click on the current plan and select the Edit Survey Programme option. See "Anticipated survey program".

Projection techniques

Uncertainty ellipsoids are projected onto a plane using two axes: the semi-major axis and the semi-minor axis. In 5D, the ellipse can be projected onto the horizontal plan or the plane perpendicular to the borehole. These projections are known as the side view and the along hole view, respectively.

Ellipses are defined by three parameters: the semi-major axis (a), the semi-minor axis (b), and the angle between the high side and the semi-minor axis (ϕ). The amount of uncertainty in the TVD at any given point is indicated by the TVD spread.

Separation factors

In order to assess the risk of a collision, anti-collision analysis calculates a separation factor for each analysis point along the primary wellpath. The separation factor is calculated by finding the minimum amount of separation between the uncertainty envelopes for the primary and secondary wellpaths. The minimum amount of separation occurs where the separation vector from the primary wellpath crosses the secondary wellpath at a right angle.

There are two methods for calculating the separation factor: the vector separation method and the pedal curve method.

The vector separation method calculates the separation factor based on the gap between the uncertainty envelopes for the primary and secondary wellpaths, along the center-to-center line. However, this calculation method can be too optimistic and miss a potential collision.

The diagrams on the left illustrate the vector separation method. Both diagrams represent the same target and planned wellpath. However, the lateral uncertainty is much larger in the second diagram, which could result in a collision.

The pedal curve method calculates the separation factor by projecting the extremities of the ellipses onto the separation vector. However, this calculation method can be too pessimistic and predict collisions that won't happen.

The diagrams on the left illustrate the pedal curve method. Both diagrams show wells that can safely be drilled without a collision. However, the second diagram shows a collision that will not happen in reality.

The table below defines the collision risk indicated by the separation factor. A high separation factor indicates a greater space between the uncertainty envelopes, and therefore, a lower risk of collision. A low separation factor indicates a smaller space between the uncertainty envelopes and a higher risk of collision.

Definitions of separation factors
Separation Factor Definition
SF ≤ 1.0 Uncertainty ellipsoids are touching or overlapping, and there is a significant risk of well path collision.
1.0 < SF ≤ 2.0 Uncertainty ellipsoids are separated, but wellpaths should be monitored.
SF > 2.0 Uncertainty ellipsoids have significant separation, collision risk minimal.

Probability of collision

The probability of collision between the uncertainty ellipsoids for the primary wellpath and a secondary wellpath is a measure of the likelihood of a collision between the wellbore. The probability of collision (P) is shown in reports and tables in the format of "1 in N", where N=1/P.
