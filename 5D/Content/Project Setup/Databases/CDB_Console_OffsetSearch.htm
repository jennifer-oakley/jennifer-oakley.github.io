About offset well search

On the Offset Search tab at the top of the Central Database window, you can perform an offset well search for wells stored in the central database. An offset well search allows you search for wells within a certain proximity of another well, which helps identify wells that may be a collision risk to the main well.

There are three methods available for identifying offset wells:

  • Site separation – Searches for wells with slots that are within a specified distance of the main well's slot. See "Site separation".
  • Well extents – Searches for wells with a well extent radius that falls within the well extent radius of the main well. See "Well extents".
  • Well bounding box – Searches for wells with a bound box that falls within the bound box of the main well. See "Well bounding box".

When you perform a search, the search results will appear on the right side of the Fields tab, under the Search Results tab. If the Auto select parent sites of wells option is selected, any wells in the same site will be selected in the data list when you select a well in the search results.

Searching for offset wells

To search for offset wells:

  1. In the Central Database window, go to the Fields tab in the lower portion of the window.
  2. Navigate to the main well.
  3. Right-click on the well name and select the Set main option.
  4. Go to the Offset Search tab at the top of the window.
  5. In the Search Method field, select the method you want to use to identify offset wells.
  6. In the Separation field, enter the desired separation distance.
  7. Click the Search button.

The search results will appear on the right side of the Fields tab, under the Search Results tab. To download a well listed in the search results, select the checkbox next to the well name and click the Download button.

Site separation

A site separation search searches for wells with slot coordinates that are within a specified distance of the main well's slot coordinate. The value in the Separation field represents the search radius, starting from the center of the slot.

Diagram illustrating a site separation search

Site separation searches rely on a suitable search radius, so that the starting locations of nearby wells that are a collision risk are detected. Because this method does not account for the extent of the primary wellpath, the minimum recommended search radius is at least twice the expected length of the main wellpath.

In the diagram on the right, the primary wellpath is shown in purple, and two secondary wellpaths are shown in yellow and green. The red circle represents the search radius for a site separation search with a search radius of 1000 m. The yellow secondary well falls within the search radius, but the green well does not. However, the green wellpath is close enough to the main wellpath to pose a potential risk. Increasing the search radius to twice the expected length of the main wellpath ensures that wells with a potential collision risk are included in the search results.

Well extents

The well extent is the distance from a well's slot to it's most distance salient point, ignoring the difference in TVD. A search radius equal to this distance will enclose the entire wellpath, when starting at the slot coordinate. The resulting well extent circle can be used to determine a potential collision risk. If the well extent circle of one well intersects with the well extent circle of another well, there may be a collision risk. However, well extent circles that do not overlap indicate that the wells are entirely separated and not at risk of a collision.

A well extents search searches for wells with a well extent circle that intersects the well extent circle of the main well. If the distance between the slot coordinates is greater than the sum of the well extent radius, then the offset well is not considered a collision risk to the main well. The value in the Separation field is an additional separation buffer that extends past the well extent radius of the main well. The recommended separation distance is twice the maximum wellpath error at total depth.

Diagram illustrating a well extents search

Well extents searches are very conservative and identify more offset wells as a potential risks than necessary. However, they also identify collision risks that may be missed by a site separation search, because they use the well extent in the search.

The diagram on the right shows the wells from the previous example, with the well extent circle for each well. The well extent distances for each well are large enough that all three well extent circles intersect one another. This means that both secondary wells are collision risks for the main well.

Well bounding box

Note

Bounding box searches are used for global AC scan.

A well's bounding box is created from the minimum and maximum northing and easting extents of the well. These well extents create a rectangle that encloses the entire wellpath. The resulting bounding box rectangles can be used to determine if there is a potential collision risk. If the well's bound box intersects with another well's bound box, it may be a collision risk. However, bounding boxes that do not overlap indicate the wells are entirely separated and not at risk of a collision.

Diagram illustrating a bounding box search

A bounding box search searches for wells with a bounding box that intersects the bounding box of the main well. If the distance between the bounding boxes is greater than the value in the Separation field, then the offset well is not considered to be a collision risk to the main well. The value in the Separation field is an additional separation buffer that extends past the bounding box of the main well. The recommended separation distance is twice the maximum wellpath error at total depth.

Bounding box searches give less conservative results than the well extents method, as they tend to enclose the wellpath much more tightly. However, a bounding box search does not consider the shape of the wellpath within the bounding box, so the search may still identify wells that are not actually a collision risk.

The diagram on the right shows the wells from the previous example, with the bounding box for each well. None of the well bounding boxes overlap. However, using a separation value of 50 m causes the green well to be flagged as a potential collision risk. The yellow well is not flagged as a collision risk, because its bounding box is 95 m from the main well's bounding box. In reality, neither well poses a collision risk, but the bounding box search has greatly reduced the number of wells that require a more detailed collision analysis.
